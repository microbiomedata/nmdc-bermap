# Comparative genomics of
                    <i>Pseudomonas syringae</i>
                    reveals convergent gene gain and loss associated with specialization onto cherry (
                    <i>Prunus avium</i>
                    )

Summary




                          Genome‐wide analyses of the effector‐ and toxin‐encoding genes were used to examine the phylogenetics and evolution of pathogenicity amongst diverse strains of
                          Pseudomonas syringae
                          causing bacterial canker of cherry (
                          Prunus avium
                          ), including pathovars

                          pv
                          morsprunorum
                          (
                          Psm
                          ) races 1 and 2,

                          pv
                          syringae

                          Pss

                          P. syringae
                          pv
                          avii
                          .
                        



                          Phylogenetic analyses revealed
                          Psm
                          races and
                          P. syringae
                          pv
                          avii
                          clades were distinct and were each monophyletic, whereas cherry‐pathogenic strains of
                          Pss
                          were interspersed amongst strains from other host species.
                        



                          A maximum likelihood approach was used to predict effectors associated with pathogenicity on cherry.
                          Pss
                          possesses a smaller repertoire of type
                          III
                          effectors but has more toxin biosynthesis clusters than
                          Psm
                          and
                          P. syringae
                          pv
                          avii

                            hop
                            AR
                            1
                          
                          and
                          
                            hop
                            BB
                            1
                          
                          through putative phage transfer and horizontal transfer respectively. By contrast, loss of the
                          
                            avrPto/hop
                            AB

                          redundant effector group was observed in cherry‐pathogenic clades. Ectopic expression of
                          
                            hop
                            AB

                          and
                          hopC1
                          triggered the hypersensitive reaction in cherry leaves, confirming computational predictions.
                        


Cherry canker provides a fascinating example of convergent evolution of pathogenicity that is explained by the mix of effector and toxin repertoires acting on a common host.
